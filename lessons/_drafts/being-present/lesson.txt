So we’d have to shoot from shore to land. We weren’t on boats because the water’s too rough in the Bering Sea in the wintertime.</p><p>And I would notice two very, very important things about these hunters.<br>One is that they never zoned out.<br>We could sit there quietly for hour after hour after hour, and nobody dulled out. Again, using this whole principle of the intelligence of the entire human being. It’s not just centered in the head, but using all of one’s senses: eyes, ears, smell, intuition, gut feel — all of these things synthesizing without thought.</p><p>That’s a concept very hard for many Westerners to understand.<br>So I was kind of immersed in that kind of way of knowing.</p><p>By age six, I decided to go out underneath the bird cliffs in Pof Islands, on St. Paul Island, where I was born.<br>We call it the <em>Galapagos of the North</em>. Tens of thousands of seabirds in what appears to be chaos, and never even a clipped wing.</p><p>And I wondered, <em>how do they do that?</em></p><p>Then an insight arose. I just intuitively went into this kind of state of being, where I’d sit there without thought.</p><p>As soon as thought drifted in, I’d zone out.<br>After sitting for hours, feeling the rhythm of the ocean and the wind against you, you could get into a dreamy state. But that state meant you wouldn’t be a good hunter, because you wouldn’t be aware.</p><p>A sea lion might come by for thirty seconds after you’ve waited all day. And you can’t afford to zone out.</p><p>I had to be fully alert, fully aware. Just like the hunters.
